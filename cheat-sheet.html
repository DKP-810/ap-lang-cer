Argument Essay — C.E.R. Cheat Sheet

AP English Language & Composition • The three moves every argument paragraph must make

C Claim

Your position — the arguable assertion your essay exists to defend.

Thesis (Overall Claim)

  • A defensible position on the prompt — not a fact, not a summary
  • Should be qualifiable: "largely," "to a significant extent," "with notable exceptions"
  • Previews your line of reasoning — the logical path your body paragraphs will follow

Body Paragraph Claims

  • Each paragraph opens with a sub-claim that supports the thesis
  • Sub-claims should build on each other, not repeat the same idea
  • Together, they form a line of reasoning — a logical progression toward your conclusion
⚠ TRAP: A thesis that simply restates the prompt or states an obvious fact ("Paine describes America") is not defensible and earns zero thesis points.

E Evidence

The concrete proof you offer to make your claim believable.

Types You Can Use

  • Historical: events, movements, Supreme Court cases, legislation
  • Current Events: recent examples from news, politics, culture
  • Personal Experience / Observation: your own life, community, school
  • Literature & Arts: novels, film, music — used as cultural evidence
  • Data & Studies: statistics, research, expert testimony
  • Analogies & Hypotheticals: comparisons that illuminate the point

What Makes Evidence Good?

  • Specific — names, dates, details — not vague gestures
  • Relevant — directly tied to the sub-claim, not tangential
  • Varied — multiple types across paragraphs shows range
⚠ TRAP: Listing evidence without explaining it = "evidence dumping." The reader doesn't do your thinking for you.

R Reasoning

The "so what?" — your explanation of why the evidence proves the claim.

What Reasoning Does

  • Connects evidence → claim: explains the logical link
  • Interprets the evidence: tells the reader what to see in it
  • Advances the argument: pushes the paragraph forward, doesn't just summarize

Test Yourself

  • After your evidence, can you answer: "This matters because…"?
  • Could someone read your evidence and reach a different conclusion? If yes, you must explain your interpretation.
  • Does your reasoning echo your sub-claim in new, deeper terms?
⚠ TRAP: Restating the evidence in different words is not reasoning. Reasoning adds a new analytical layer.

Counterargument & Rebuttal

Acknowledging the other side — then explaining why your position still holds.

Two Moves

  • Concession: Acknowledge what the opposition gets right. ("It is true that…" / "Admittedly…")
  • Rebuttal: Explain why that doesn't undermine your argument. ("However…" / "Yet this overlooks…")

Why It Matters

  • Shows you've considered complexity, not just one side
  • Often the fastest path to the sophistication point
  • Strongest when woven into body paragraphs, not isolated in a throwaway paragraph
⚠ TRAP: A counterargument you never rebut actually weakens your essay. Always answer it.

✦ The Sophistication Point

  • Situate the argument in a broader context (historical, social, cultural)
  • Explore tension or complexity within your own position
  • Use vivid, purposeful style — not just "fancy" words, but language that serves meaning
  • Develop a nuanced thesis (qualified, not absolute)
  • Make an insightful connection across evidence or time periods

Essay at a Glance

1Intro: Context → Thesis with defensible claim + line of reasoning
2Body ¶1: Sub-claim → Evidence → Reasoning (your strongest, most intuitive point)
3Body ¶2: Sub-claim → Evidence → Reasoning (builds complexity, deepens the argument)
4Body ¶3: Counterargument + Rebuttal, OR a third sub-claim that adds nuance
5Conclusion: Extend — don't repeat. Connect to a bigger idea or implication.
